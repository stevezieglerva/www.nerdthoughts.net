--- layout: post title: Good Free Geocoding Service from Texas A&M date: '2014-04-26T09:13:00.001-04:00' author: Stephen Ziegler tags: modified_time: '2014-04-26T09:14:13.714-04:00' thumbnail: http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-X2Qphkwhe3M/U1urCKOLUgI/AAAAAAAAg4Y/zuHRFg3kyX0/s72-c/tamu_1.png blogger_id: tag:blogger.com,1999:blog-1727955271917225446.post-4812128235209524537 blogger_orig_url: http://www.nerdthoughts.net/2014/04/good-free-geocoding-service-from-texas-a.html --- If you're an amateur GIS hack, getting lots of addresses geocoded to lat/long for free can be a pain. Several websites offer the service for free, but they are often throttled down and have daily limits.

I ran into Texas A&M's GeoServices that is a really nice service. They have different offerings including a partner program that offers up the service for free assuming you're not using it for commercial purposes and you give them some props on your website.

 


  • They allow you to upload "databases" of geodata.  The databases can be tab-delimited text, CSV, or Access DBs. For geocoding, your file needs to have columns for Street, City, State, and Zip.



  • You can then kick off geoprocessing. It takes about 3 minutes to process 1,000 addresses (at least when running it on the weekend). They have a nice set of auto-refresh pages that allow you to see progress.




  • The resulting file will have the lat/longs plus some information on the accuracy of the match. For real GIS nerds, I'm not sure how accurate the geocoding is compared to Google, Bing, and Here. But, it's good enough for me.  Once you have your data, throw it into ArcGIS or QGIS and have at it.


So, my one big beef with the site, and it's a big one, is that they don't use SSL. So, when logging in, updating your password, and using the site, all of the web traffic is in clear text. You should probably assume that your password is stored in clear text.
