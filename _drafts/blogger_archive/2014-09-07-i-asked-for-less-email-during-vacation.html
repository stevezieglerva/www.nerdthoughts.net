In August, I took a week of vacation and warned co-workers that I would not be checking email. On days off, I often read email, especially if I'm in town or there is a lot of proposal activity.  But, for that vacation week, I was completely disconnected from work. Here's the request I sent to just my ICF colleagues (clients and spammers received no warning, just my OOTO email response):
"Just a reminder that I’ll be on vacation next week and not checking emails. This year, I’ve averaged 66 emails per calendar day.  Please consider leaving me off of CC’d emails since I expect to have 450+ emails when I return.  If it’s important, we can catch up with a call when I return.  Thanks. :) "
So, when I returned, what did I find?  A 55% reduction in email that week. Instead of my typical 66 emails per day, I averaged 29.  Here's the breakdown of emails the week before and during vacation:



I was curious if just my normal heavy emailers sent less, so I also counted the emails by sender importance.  I've implemented a variation of Scott Hanselman's Three Most Important Outlook Rules For Processing Mail so it's easy for me to see what types of people were sending me email:


People in my Important and Less Important (primary audience for my request for less email) groups both sent less.  Those in my Junk category continued their normal, relentless emails.

Then, I was curious if this drop was significant.  It felt like lot a less email than normal, but I wasn't sure. I index my email in Elasticsearch and visualize it in Kibana.  So, I can easily get lots of stats on my email. I counted the number of emails per day for all of 2014 and then created a 7-day moving average to smooth out the weekend dips. I also added control band lines for the average and one standard deviation above and below. Just with a quick glance, you can see that the dip in August is dramatic. It drops almost a standard deviation in the week.  But, you'll also see that my email volume has some pretty wide variations. I'm sure there's a statistical way to determine if the vacation drop was significant, but for now, I'll trust my eyes that my email request did reduce my email volume.



So, what happened the week after my vacation? My colleagues came back with a vengeance (especially that first Monday) and I averaged 77 emails per day!


While I'll never be able to fully stem the email tide, grouping my email into buckets of importance definitely helps wading through them.  It took me a lot less time to get through my 212 vacation emails than I expected, especially since 20% of my junk email (including junk work email) was filtered out.
